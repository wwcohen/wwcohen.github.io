A gentle introduction to matrices

If you're not familiar with matrices, they are simply a convenient and widely-used way of organizing lots of additionals and multiplications.

Here's a more down-to-earth example of when you might use matrices. Raphael needs get three items, which I'll abbreviate (a, b, c), in different quantities (6, 1, 1). These items are available at two different stores for different prices. Which store will be cheaper for Raphael to do his shopping?

This is an easy question to solve, as shown above: if he does the math, Raphael will discover that he would pay $1.20 less at Food Lion (f) than Giant Eagle (e). The computation he needs to do is just to multiple the quantity needed for each item on his list by the cost in a store, and then add these numbers up, for each store.

A matrix is just a table of numbers. We can write down all the numbers we need here in two matrices: $$ \textbf{r} = \left[ 6 ~ 1 ~ 1 \right], ~~~ \textbf{P} = \left[ \begin{array}{rr}1.00 & 0.80 \\ 1.50 & 1.00 \\ 4.00 & 4.50 \end{array} \right] $$ If a matrix has just one row, like the one I labeled r above, then it's called "row vector", and if it has just one column, it's called called a "column vector". I will usually use boldfaced lower-case letters for matrices that are vectors and boldfaced upper-case letters otherwise.

Let's give these numbers names so we can follow the operations we need to do easily. I will use \( r_a \) for the number of apples Raphael needs, use use \( p_{a, e} \) for the price of apples at Giant Eagle'sand so on. So then $$ \textbf{r} = \left[ 6 ~ 1 ~ 1 \right] = \left[ r_a ~ r_b ~ r_c \right] $$ and $$ \textbf{P} = \left[ \begin{array}{rr} 1.00 & 0.80 \\ 1.50 & 1.00 \\ 4.00 & 4.50 \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{rr} p_{a,e} & p_{a,f} \\ p_{b,e} & p_{b,f} \\ p_{c,e} & c_{c,f} \end{array} \right] $$ And the computation we want to do is to find the total price for Raphael in each store, which if we wrote out as a row vector would be $$ \left[ (r_a p_{a,e} + r_b p_{b,e} + r_c p_{c,e}) ~~~~ (r_a p_{a,f} + r_b p_{b,f} + r_c p_{c,f}) \right] $$

There are other ways that matrices can be combined. One is just to stick them together side-by-side, or "stack" them: so if Raphael was going to shop with Shaun and Shaun's shopping list was the same except with no apples I might combine those two lists into a matrix Q like this $$ \textbf{Q} = \left[ \begin{array}{rrr}6 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array} \right] $$ (Notice I need to add zeros to Shaun's list to make it the same length as Raphael's, so I can combine them.)
